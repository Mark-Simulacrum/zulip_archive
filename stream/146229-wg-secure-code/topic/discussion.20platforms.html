@XAMPPRocky Regarding using Reddit a platform for getting input from the community: I'll explain where I'm coming from and why I chose to host this discussion on Reddit, and then we'll hopefully be able to come up with a better solution.

Off the top of my head, here are some of the requirements the discussion platform has to fulfill:

  1. Reach the intended audience and enable it to participate in the discussion with little effort, meaning:
    A. The platform should be one that your intended audience frequents, or can be easily directed to
    B. Not have a prohibitively high entry barrier for participation. In fact I'd advocate for as low entry barrier as possible
    C. If the opinion you share has already been stated, require as little effort as possible to express your support

  2. Structure the discussion in a scalable way, meaning:
    A. Provide a way for discussion participants to talk to each other and come up with new ideas in the course of it
    B. Avoid requiring people to restate the same point many times if it has already been stated
    C. Allow following different discussion threads easily and/or skipping certain parts that are not relevant to the aspects of the discussion that you're interested in
    D. Encourage people to lay out their opinions in a concise fashion (necessary to fulfill the above points)
    E. Conceal or remove posts or conversation threads that are not constructive

Email completely fails 1C and 2A. Discourse, Github and Zulip fail 2C, which in turn makes 1B, 1C, 2A and 2B really hard in large discussions. Imagine https://redd.it/gidtpe linearized and sorted by date - it's a mess! Platforms like Reddit and Disqus (not to be confused with Discourse) are so much better at 2C thanks to "every reply starts a thread" system, and fulfilling 2C enables a great deal of other points in turn. There are also platforms entirely dedicated to decision-making and laying out pros and cons, but almost no-one is familiar with them so the entry barrier is quite high.

I can understand the reluctance to use an platform that you don't fully control and/or that is perceived as informal (e.g. Facebook) for official decisions, but for pre-RFCs Reddit seems to be the least terrible option that we currently have. So what are the requirements that Reddit does not currently meet? Once we have that written down, we'll hopefully find the crux of the disagreement and be able to figure out a better solution.
