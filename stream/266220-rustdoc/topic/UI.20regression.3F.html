Aaron Loucks (Feb 20 2022 at 17:13):

In response to the "universally disliked" comment: the PR had all downvotes. I posted the complaint mostly due to feeling panicked about the way things are moving forward in rustdoc. As someone who has weird visual processing glitches, having items grouped by type and color coded greatly improves my ability to process things. For example, seeing the green text on an item in a function signature is a visual cue that the type of object is an enum. Having the enums and traits grouped separately also provides similar visual cues.

I thought it was ironic that the motivation of these changes was to help people with aspersers or other accessibility challenges, but the changes seem to have the opposite effect. I strongly rely on syntax coloring, consistent formatting, patterns and grouping in order to process things in a meaningful way and these changes have a negative impact on my ability to be productive.

jsha (Feb 22 2022 at 06:37):

Thanks for the additional context. And I'm really sorry the changes have made you feel panicked! In particular it's useful to know that your visual glitches are particularly helped by having a wider variety of colors. We can't necessarily design a UI that will work perfectly for everybody, but I think on balance the reduced number of colors winds up being the right call.

Aaron Loucks (Feb 22 2022 at 15:02):

The decision feels arbitrary. Why would we use color and grouping for other distinctions but not here? We give items that are fundamentally different distinct colors and groups, but suddenly enums are now the same as structs?

Many of the suggestions on issues 59845 and 59829 are steps backward. Removing visual cues is not the same as removing clutter. For example, the background on code blocks allows the user to scan the page and easily find code examples. The code blocks stand out. There was a statement about Go and Python docs being used as examples, but the current/previous rustdoc output is considerably nicer than both Go and Python docs.

The design guidelines for autism spectrum recommend reducing contrast, they don't recommend reducing the number of colors. In fact it's the opposite.

From:
https://www.fullfabric.com/articles/how-to-design-visual-learning-resources-for-neurodiverse-students

The W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) reports that dyslexic individuals respond well to colour differences among background and text. The WAI also tests different text and background colour contrasts to decipher the most viable combinations.

While visual supports form the basis of facilitating learning in autistic individuals, getting the right visuals involves a combination of artistry and strategy. Colour can add meaning, clarity and dimension to visual materials; it can bring order and truth to the visual learner.

When it comes to colours, neurodivergent students tend to prefer muted and pastel hues and neutral tones. Sensory Differences: Online Training Module by J Rogers and J Short asserts that neurodivergent students particularly favour tranquil greens and blues.

I applaud your effort to make rustdocs more accessible, and I believe that the changes are well intended, but I feel strongly that some of the changes are moving in the wrong direction and are having a negative impact.
